The Internal Assessment (IA) is a design portfolio showing your complete process: understanding a user problem, defining requirements, generating and testing ideas, and presenting a final redesign. You work for approximately 50 hours. It is worth 40% of your final DP grade.

The Five Criteria at a Glance

Criterion Name Marks What You Are Showing
A Empathize 9 Research into the user, context, and existing products
B Define 6 A clear problem statement and a testable design specification
C Ideation 6 Three distinct design directions, evaluated against your specification
D Design 6 Iterative models/prototypes and formal technical drawings
E Present 6 Final solution compared directly to the original product
Total 33 Submitted as a digital portfolio

Key Rules You Must Follow

📄
Maximum 35 A4 pages. Examiners stop reading after page 35.
✏️
Annotations are limited to 10 words each. Annotations over 10 words count toward your word limit.
✍️
Handwritten annotations must be clear and legible, equivalent to Arial 11pt.
🔢
Maximum 3,500 words. This applies to body text, not annotations under 10 words.
🚫
No appendices. Examiners will not read them. Include everything in the main 35 pages.
🔁
You must redesign an existing product, not invent a completely new one.
👤
The redesign must address the needs of a real, named intended user.
📚
Use subject-specific vocabulary throughout (ergonomics, anthropometrics, biomechanics, etc.).
⚠ Critical Rule — Redesign, Do Not Invent

You must redesign an existing product. Do not invent a new product from scratch. The IB exemplar "FPV Drone Racing Tool" (Example 5) lost marks because the student tried to create a new multi-tool, instead of redesigning the tool bag that was the actual identified problem. Start with a product that already exists — your job is to improve it.

Criterion A is the foundation of the entire project. Here you research the real user, analyze the context of use, and examine existing products. All later criteria must connect back to what you find here.

Three Strands

1
Primary Persona ~2 pages
A detailed profile of your real intended user, based on observation and interview.
2
Storyboard ~3 pages
A step-by-step visual breakdown of the specific task where the existing product fails the user.
3
Existing Product Analysis ~4 pages · 4–6 products
Analysis of 4–6 existing products using formal design principles and human factors.

Mark Scheme

MarksWhat the Examiner Is Looking For
0No work submitted, or work that does not meet the level 1 descriptor.
1–3Basic research with limited connection to the user or the existing product.
4–6Research that identifies a problem but lacks depth in human factors analysis or product critique.
7–9Thorough, user-centred research across all three strands. Human factors (anthropometrics, biomechanics, ergonomics) are used. Existing product analysis is critical and specific. All findings directly connect to the user's problem.

The persona is a detailed profile of one real person — not an imaginary character. You should observe and interview this person while they use the existing product. Photographs of them using the product are expected.

The persona must show: who the person is, what they want to achieve, what specifically frustrates them about the existing product, and the physical or environmental context of use.

Persona Outline — Copy This Structure

[NAME OF PERSONA]
- Photo/s of the person using the existing product
- Age:
- Occupation:
- Location:
- Relevant physical characteristics or limitations:

GOALS
- [What does this person want to achieve?]
- [Be specific to the task your product addresses]

FRUSTRATIONS
- [What specifically annoys or limits them about the existing product?]
- [Use direct quotes from your interview if possible]

MOTIVATIONS
- [Why do they care about solving this problem?]

CONTEXT OF USE
- [Where, when, and how often do they use the product?]
- [What is their environment like?]

HUMAN FACTORS
- Relevant anthropometric data (e.g., hand dimensions, percentile range)
- Relevant biomechanical considerations (e.g., grip force, reach range)
- Ergonomic requirements (e.g., posture, repetitive strain risk)
What Success Looks Like

The Prosthetics project scored 8/9 on Criterion A. The persona interview revealed a specific, measurable goal: "The hand can move like it did before 80–90%." This specific target gave the student a clear, testable specification to work toward in Criteria B and D.

Common Problems
  • The persona is fictional or based on a general stereotype rather than a real, interviewed person.
  • The frustrations are general ("it is uncomfortable") rather than specific to a particular moment of product use.
  • No connection is made between the persona's frustrations and the design of the existing product.
  • No photographs of the real user with the real product are included.
  • Human factors (anthropometrics, biomechanics) are completely absent.

The storyboard is a step-by-step visual analysis of the specific task where the existing product fails. It is not a general story about the user's life. It is a close analysis of one particular moment of failure or frustration.

Each frame should show one step in the task sequence. Annotations should explain what is happening and why it is a problem. The final frames should make clear what the consequence of the failure is for the user.

Storyboard Frame Structure — Copy This

STORYBOARD: [Task Name]
Persona: [Name from your persona]
Existing Product: [Product name and model if known]

FRAME 1: [Title of step]
- Visual: [What is shown — drawing or photo]
- Annotation: [What is happening and why it is a problem]

FRAME 2: [Title of step]
- Visual: [What is shown]
- Annotation: [Key action or difficulty at this step]

FRAME 3: [Title of step — the moment of failure]
- Visual: [The specific point where the product fails]
- Annotation: [What goes wrong and what the consequence is for the user]

FRAME 4–6: [Continue breaking down the task]

CONCLUSION FRAME:
- What the user cannot do, or what takes too long / causes pain
- Connection to: which design specification points this will generate
What Success Looks Like

The Prosthetics project storyboard focused narrowly on a single failing task: braking a bicycle. Each frame showed one specific moment in that task. This gave the examiner a clear understanding of the precise problem to be solved.

Common Problems
  • The storyboard shows a general lifestyle (e.g., "refugee daily life") instead of analyzing one specific task step-by-step.
  • The Math Play Set project scored only 4/9 because its storyboard showed general difficulties rather than a detailed task analysis. Do not make this mistake.
  • Frames show what the user does, but do not explain why each step is a problem with the existing product.
  • The storyboard uses only photographs without analytical annotations — annotations are required.

Analyze 4–6 existing products in this category. This should include the product your persona already uses, plus competing or related products. Your analysis should be critical — find both strengths and weaknesses.

Use formal design terminology: function, aesthetics, ergonomics, materials, sustainability, safety, cultural considerations, inclusivity, anthropometrics. Do not just describe what you see — evaluate and justify your assessment.

What Success Looks Like

Strong product analysis connects each product's weakness directly to the user's frustrations identified in the persona. Each product teaches you something specific that will influence your design specification in Criterion B.

Common Problems
  • Analyzing only 3 products — the guide recommends 4–6.
  • Analysis that only describes the product without evaluating it critically.
  • No connection between the products analyzed and the persona's specific needs.
  • Human factors (anthropometrics, biomechanics, percentile data) are completely missing.
  • Only the product the persona uses is analyzed — you must look at competing products too.

Criterion B converts your research into a precise, testable document. It has two parts: a short problem statement that summarizes the design challenge, and a design specification that lists all the requirements your redesign must meet.

Two Strands

1
Problem Statement ~1 page
A concise, specific description of the problem your redesign will solve, focused on one user and one product.
2
Design Specification ~3 pages
A table of specific, measurable requirements each justified by evidence from Criterion A.

Mark Scheme

MarksWhat the Examiner Is Looking For
0No work submitted.
1–2A problem statement exists but is vague. Specification points are general wishes, not testable requirements.
3–4A reasonable problem statement. Most specification points are reasonable but some lack justification or measurable targets.
5–6A precise problem statement focused on one user and one existing product. Every specification point is measurable and fully justified by evidence from Criterion A. The specification is clearly a test plan for the final solution, not a wish list.

The problem statement is a short, focused paragraph. It should name your user, name the existing product, and state the specific problem that prevents the user from achieving their goal. It should not describe a global issue or a general category of problems.

Problem Statement Template — Copy This

[Persona name], a [describe user in one phrase], currently uses [name of existing product] to [describe the task].

However, [name of existing product] fails to [specific limitation], which means that [persona name] cannot [specific goal from persona] without [specific difficulty or consequence].

This project will redesign [name of existing product] to address [specific problem], so that [persona name] can [specific user goal from persona].

The redesigned product must meet the following design specification.
Common Problems
  • The problem statement describes a large social issue (e.g., "millions of people worldwide suffer from...") instead of focusing on one user and one product.
  • The statement does not name the existing product that will be redesigned.
  • The statement introduces the solution before the specification has been written.

The design specification is a structured table of requirements. Each requirement must be:

  • Specific — not vague. "The grip diameter must be between 30mm and 40mm" is good. "It must be comfortable" is not.
  • Measurable — you must be able to test whether the final design meets this requirement.
  • Justified — each requirement must link to evidence from Criterion A (persona interview, storyboard finding, or product analysis).
⚠ Critical Insight — Trowel Project Commentary

The IB Trowel project commentary specifically criticized specifications that referenced "boarding houses and aesthetics" without any supporting evidence from Criterion A. Every specification point must trace back to something you found in your research. If you cannot find a source in Criterion A, do not include that specification point.

Design Specification Table Template — Copy This

DESIGN SPECIFICATION

Spec # | Requirement | Justification | Source (Criterion A) | Priority
-------|-------------|---------------|----------------------|----------
1      | [What the product must do or be. Include measurable targets where possible.] | [Why is this required? What did your research show?] | [Persona / Storyboard frame # / Product analysis #] | Essential

2      | [e.g., The handle diameter must be between 32mm and 38mm to suit the 5th–95th percentile female hand width.] | [Persona interview: user reported grip fatigue. Product analysis showed handle is 28mm — below 5th percentile range.] | Persona + Product 1 analysis | Essential

3      | [e.g., The product must be operated using one hand only.] | [Storyboard frame 3: user's other hand is occupied with the bike brake lever.] | Storyboard, Frame 3 | Essential

4      | [A desirable feature] | [Supporting evidence] | [Source] | Desirable

Note: "Essential" means the final design fails if this is not met. "Desirable" means it would improve the design but is not a failure if absent.
Common Problems
  • Specifications are a general wish list ("it should look good", "it should be safe") with no measurable targets.
  • No column for the source in Criterion A — making it impossible to verify the justification.
  • All specifications are marked "Essential" when some are clearly preferences.
  • Specifications are not tested against in Criterion C or Criterion E — making them useless as a test plan.

Criterion C requires you to generate three genuinely different design directions and then evaluate all three against your design specification. The goal is to show that you explored options before committing to one.

Two Strands

1
Three Feasible Design Ideas ~6 pages · 1–2 pages each
Three distinct design directions, each shown with annotated sketches or models and key features described.
2
Evaluation Against Specification ~2 pages
A structured evaluation (matrix or equivalent) testing all three ideas against every specification point, with a clear final selection and justification.

Mark Scheme

MarksWhat the Examiner Is Looking For
0No work submitted.
1–2Ideas presented but they are similar variations, or evaluation is personal opinion without reference to the specification.
3–4Three reasonably distinct ideas. Partial evaluation against the specification but not all points tested.
5–6Three genuinely distinct ideas. A structured evaluation matrix covers ALL ideas against EVERY specification point. The selected idea is clearly identified and the selection is justified by the evaluation evidence.

Each idea must be genuinely different — not just the same solution with a different colour or a slightly different shape. Think about different approaches to solving the problem: different mechanisms, materials, configurations, or interaction methods.

Idea Presentation Template (for each of 3 ideas) — Copy This

DESIGN IDEA [#]: [Give the idea a descriptive name]

CONCEPT DESCRIPTION
- In 2–3 sentences, describe the design approach and how it is different from the others.

KEY FEATURES
- Feature 1: [Describe and annotate]
- Feature 2: [Describe and annotate]
- Feature 3: [Describe and annotate]

HOW IT ADDRESSES THE PROBLEM
- [Link back to a specific persona frustration or storyboard finding]

MATERIALS (proposed)
- Primary material:
- Why this material:

INITIAL FEASIBILITY
- Can this be made within the constraints of the project?
- Any risks or difficulties with this approach?

VISUAL REPRESENTATION
- [Annotated sketches, drawings, or photographs of a quick model]
Common Problems
  • Three ideas that are all the same concept with minor differences (e.g., three handles with different grip textures).
  • Ideas that do not connect to the specification or persona frustrations.
  • Sketches without annotations — the ideas must be explained, not just shown.
  • Ideas that introduce completely new products not related to the original identified problem.
Key Insight — Prosthetics Project (6/6)

The Prosthetics project earned full marks in Criterion C because it created a complete evaluation matrix covering ALL three ideas against EVERY specification point. It then stated clearly which idea was selected and provided a specific justification based on the matrix results — not on personal preference.

The evaluation matrix should test every idea against every specification point from Criterion B. Use a scale (e.g., ✓ = meets requirement, ~ = partially meets, ✗ = does not meet) or a score (0–2 or 0–5) for each cell. After the matrix, write a clear conclusion selecting one idea.

Evaluation Matrix Template — Copy This

EVALUATION MATRIX

Spec # | Specification Point (abbreviated) | Idea 1 | Idea 2 | Idea 3
-------|-------------------------------------|--------|--------|--------
1      | [e.g., One-hand operation]          |   ✓    |   ✓    |   ✗
2      | [e.g., Handle diameter 32–38mm]     |   ✓    |   ~    |   ✓
3      | [e.g., Operates in wet conditions]  |   ~    |   ✓    |   ✓
4      | [e.g., Weight under 200g]           |   ✓    |   ✗    |   ✓
5      | [Continue for ALL spec points]      |        |        |
...

TOTALS:  ✓ = _ / _ | ✗ = _ / _  ||  ✓ = _ / _ | ✗ = _ / _ ||  ✓ = _ / _ | ✗ = _ / _

SELECTION
Selected idea: Idea [#] — [Name]
Justification: [Explain using the matrix results. Which specification points does this idea best satisfy? Why was this more important than what the other ideas offered? Did you consult your persona/user in this decision?]
Common Problems
  • Evaluation based on personal opinion ("I prefer Idea 2 because it looks nicer") rather than on the specification.
  • Matrix that does not include all specification points — incomplete evaluation.
  • No clear conclusion identifying which idea was selected.
  • The selected idea does not match the matrix results — a contradiction that loses marks.
  • No evidence that the user/persona was consulted during the evaluation.

Criterion D is about showing your development process, not just a final product. You must show multiple models or prototypes, document what each test revealed, and show how you changed your design in response. Then you must produce formal technical drawings of your final design.

Two Strands

1
Iterative Fidelity Models ~6 pages
Multiple prototypes at increasing fidelity. Each must be tested, with the result documented and a change made for the next version.
2
Technical Drawings ~4 pages
Formal orthographic drawings, assembled and exploded views, with full dimensions and a Bill of Materials.

Mark Scheme

MarksWhat the Examiner Is Looking For
0No work submitted.
1–2One prototype shown, or models shown without testing evidence. Technical drawings missing or very incomplete.
3–4Some iteration evident. Testing documented but changes between prototypes are not clearly explained. Technical drawings partially complete.
5–6Clear model–test–refine cycle across multiple prototypes. Each test result directly drives the next change. Testing involves the user/persona. Technical drawings are complete: orthographic, assembled, exploded, dimensions, and Bill of Materials.
Key Insight — Prosthetics Project

The Prosthetics project excelled in Criterion D by documenting each prototype in a structured table: Prototype Number → Testing Result → How to Improve for the Next Prototype. This format makes iteration evidence impossible to miss.

Iteration Log Template — Copy This

ITERATIVE DEVELOPMENT LOG

PROTOTYPE 1 — [Descriptive name, e.g., "Low-fidelity foam model"]
- Material/method: [What was it made from and how?]
- Spec points tested: [Which specification points did this test address?]
- Testing method: [How was it tested? With the user? With measurements?]
- Result: [What did the test show? Be specific. Use measurements or user feedback quotes.]
- Decision: [What will you change for the next prototype and why?]
- [Photograph or scan of the model]

PROTOTYPE 2 — [Descriptive name, e.g., "Revised handle grip — 3D printed"]
- Material/method:
- Spec points tested:
- Testing method:
- Result:
- Decision:
- [Photograph or scan]

PROTOTYPE 3 — [Final pre-production model]
- Material/method:
- Spec points tested:
- Result:
- Final decision: [Is the design ready? What, if anything, still needs refinement?]
- [Photograph or scan]
Common Problems
  • One polished prototype with no evidence of earlier, rougher models — this shows no iteration.
  • Models shown in photographs but no testing results or changes documented.
  • Development that does not address the original specification — the specification must guide the testing.
  • No involvement of the real user in testing the prototypes.
  • Changes between prototypes are not explained — just showing two different models is not enough.

Technical drawings communicate the final design with precision. They must be formal, using standard engineering drawing conventions. Sketches are not acceptable here — these drawings must be made with instruments or CAD software.

Required Drawing Types

  • Orthographic projection — Front, side, and top views with full dimensions
  • Assembled view — Showing how the product looks when complete
  • Exploded view — Showing all components separated, with labels and part numbers
  • Section view (if applicable) — For complex internal features

Bill of Materials Template — Copy This

BILL OF MATERIALS

Part # | Part Name | Material | Quantity | Dimensions | Manufacturing Process | Source/Notes
-------|-----------|----------|----------|------------|-----------------------|-------------
1      | [e.g., Main body shell] | [e.g., ABS plastic] | 1 | [e.g., 120 × 45 × 30mm] | [e.g., FDM 3D print] | [e.g., In-house]
2      | [Part name] | [Material] | [Qty] | [Dimensions] | [Process] | [Notes]
3      | [Part name] | [Material] | [Qty] | [Dimensions] | [Process] | [Notes]
...

Notes:
- Dimensions should match those shown in the orthographic drawings.
- Manufacturing process should be realistic for a student project.
Common Problems
  • Drawings without dimensions — all measurements must be labeled.
  • Only a perspective or 3D render provided, with no orthographic views.
  • No exploded view — this is required to show component relationships.
  • Bill of Materials is missing or lists parts not shown in the drawings.
  • Drawings show a different design from the one developed in the iteration stage.

Criterion E is the final section of your portfolio. Here you present your completed redesign and compare it directly to the original product you identified in Criterion A. The examiner wants to see clear evidence that your redesign solves the problems you found in your research.

Two Strands

1
Presenting the Redesigned Solution ~2 pages
High-quality visuals of the final product with annotation callouts identifying key features and design decisions.
2
Comparing with the Original Product ~2 pages
A direct before-and-after comparison using photographs, feature tables, and evidence from user testing.

Mark Scheme

MarksWhat the Examiner Is Looking For
0No work submitted.
1–2Final design shown but not annotated. No direct comparison with the original product.
3–4Annotated final design. Some comparison with the original, but not structured or evidence-based.
5–6Clearly annotated final design. Direct side-by-side comparison with the original product using photographs, a feature/specification table, and user testing evidence. The comparison connects explicitly to the problem identified in Criterion A.

Use high-quality photographs of the final physical model (or high-quality renders if a physical model was not possible). Each key feature must be annotated. Annotations should explain what the feature does and which specification point it satisfies.

Final Design Annotation Structure — Copy This

FINAL DESIGN — [Product Name]

[Photograph/render — multiple angles recommended]

ANNOTATION CALLOUTS (keep each to 10 words or fewer):
A → [Feature name]: [What it does / spec point met]
B → [Feature name]: [What it does / spec point met]
C → [Feature name]: [What it does / spec point met]
D → [Material label]: [Material used and reason]
E → [Dimension label]: [Key measurement]

KEY DESIGN DECISIONS
(Use this for longer explanations — these count toward word limit)

Decision 1: [Feature]
- Why this feature: [Link to persona frustration or spec point]
- How it was developed: [Reference prototype number from Criterion D]

Decision 2: [Feature]
- Why this feature:
- How it was developed:

USER RESPONSE
- [Persona name]'s response when tested with the final model:
- Specific feedback quote if available:
Common Problems
  • Beautiful renders with no annotation callouts — visuals alone do not earn marks.
  • Annotations that only name a feature without connecting it to a specification point or persona need.
  • New features introduced here that were not tested in Criterion D — this breaks the design narrative.
  • New materials specified here that were not tested in any prototype.
Key Insight — Trowel and Prosthetics Commentaries

Both the Trowel and Prosthetics exemplar commentaries specifically note that comparing the redesigned product directly to the original product identified in Criterion A is essential for high marks. Use side-by-side photographs, a feature comparison table, and evidence from user testing. Do not just describe your design — show how it improves on what came before.

-------|-------------|------|--------

Common Problems
  • No direct comparison with the original product from Criterion A — this is the most common mistake in Criterion E.
  • Comparison is only written text with no visual side-by-side evidence.
  • No user testing results — the user must interact with both products for the comparison to be meaningful.
  • Specification compliance is not addressed — the examiner needs to see which spec points were met.
  • Not acknowledging specification points that were not fully met — honest evaluation is expected and respected.
