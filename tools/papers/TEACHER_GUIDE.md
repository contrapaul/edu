# Regulatory Papers, Please — Teacher Guide

A browser game where students take a product from concept to market by navigating
real-world safety and compliance testing. No install, no login, saves locally.
**Play:** `edu.contrapaul.com/tools/papers`

---

## What students do

Each student runs a small product company (one of three) and shepherds products
through six phases: **Brief → Design → Testing → Certification → Manufacturing →
Launch.** Choices compound — a cheap component sourced in Design resurfaces as a
failed test, a certification discrepancy, a customs hold, or a product recall.

- **Mina / LuminaTech** — consumer electronics (EMC, wireless, eye-safety)
- **Leo / AeroCube** — drones & RC toys (mechanical/impact safety, child safety, aviation & privacy)
- **Samira / Biome Solutions** — sustainable home goods (food-contact, environmental-claim substantiation)

A typical product takes **15–30 minutes**; a full company line is roughly a lesson or two.

| 3 — Modelling | Prototype & pre-test decisions drive later outcomes |

| 5 — Innovation & markets | Target-market selection, certification cost vs market access |
| Human factors / safety | EMC, flammability, drop/impact, battery, food-contact, eye-safety testing |

**Standards students will meet (real ones):** FCC Part 15, CE (EMC/RED/LVD),
RoHS, WEEE, UL, CCC, PSE, RCM, Prop 65, ASTM F963 / EU Toy Directive, UN 38.3,
FAA Remote ID, GDPR, IEC 62471, FDA 21 CFR & EC 1935/2004 (food contact), and
FTC Green Guides. The in-game **Regulatory Library** (📋) explains each in plain
language and is a usable revision resource.

## Suggested lesson flow

1. **Hook (5 min)** — project the title screen; ask: *"What has to be true before a product is legally allowed on a shelf?"*
2. **Guided first product (25–35 min)** — class plays one Mina Smart Speaker together, pausing at each phase to discuss the trade-off.
3. **Independent play** — students complete a full company line, keeping a decision log.
4. **Debrief** — compare grades and, crucially, *why*: who cut a corner where, and what it cost downstream.

---

## Assessment ideas

- **Decision journal** — document each major choice and justify it with the relevant standard.
- **Comparison report** — play the same product twice (cheap vs. compliant sourcing); compare cost, time-to-market, and grade.
- **Recall case study** — find a real product recall, then identify which game phase the failure maps to.
- **Design a Product 4** — students spec a hypothetical next product for their company, listing the markets, standards, tests, and likely failure points.

---

## Teaching notes

- **There's no single "right" path.** Premium choices cost budget; cheap ones cost later. A high grade requires balancing certification success, budget, time, customer satisfaction, and sustainability — mirror this in discussion.
- **The humour is intentional** (absurd emails, "DEFINITELY CERTIFIED ELECTRONICS CO."). It keeps a dry topic engaging; the underlying standards are accurate.
- **Sandbox panel (🌐)** lets stronger students explore the economics — global shortages, opening factories, hiring — as an extension.
- **Saving:** progress is stored in the browser (localStorage). "New Company" starts over; there is no account or server.
- **Accessibility:** fully keyboard-playable; mini-games use ✓/✕ markers (not colour alone) and screen-reader labels; works on phones.

> Content note: the regulations are modelled for teaching and kept faithful to real
> frameworks, but the game is a learning tool, not legal/compliance advice.
