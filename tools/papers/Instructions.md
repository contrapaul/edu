We need to teach design thinking, material properties, manufacturing processes, and global standards while keeping the gameplay loop satisfying and substantial. 


# "Regulatory Papers, Please"
## Comprehensive Game Design & Development Plan

---

## 1. GAME OVERVIEW & LEARNING OBJECTIVES

### Target Audience
- DP Design Technology students (ages 14-16)
- Grade 9-10 general design/technology classes
- Play time: 2-4 hours per full character path (45-90 minutes per product)

### Learning Objectives
By completing all three character paths, students will understand:

| Concept | Application |
|---------|-------------|
| Material properties & selection | Swapping materials based on mechanical, thermal, electrical, and regulatory requirements |
| Manufacturing processes | Injection molding vs. CNC vs. 3D printing trade-offs |
| International standards | CE, FCC, RoHS, Prop 65, CCC, ISO, ASTM, EN standards |
| Testing & certification | EMC testing, mechanical safety, chemical composition, flammability |
| Product lifecycle | Design → Prototype → Test → Redesign → Certify → Manufacture → Ship |
| Economic decision-making | Budget trade-offs, time-to-market, market access decisions |

## 3. EXTENDED PRODUCT DEVELOPMENT LIFECYCLE

Each product goes through **6 distinct phases**. No phase can be skipped, and each requires meaningful player interaction. This ensures a single product takes at minimum 15-25 minutes for a focused player, and 30-45 minutes for a thorough student.

### Phase 1: Product Brief & Requirements Gathering (3-5 minutes)

**Objective:** Define what you're building and where you'll sell it.

**Player Actions:**
1. Read the product concept memo from your team (narrative introduction with humor)
2. Select target markets from a world map (each adds specific requirements):
   - ✓ USA: FCC, UL/ETL, ASTM (if applicable), California (optional but lucrative)
   - ✓ EU: CE Mark (multiple directives), RoHS, WEEE
   - ✓ China: CCC
   - ✓ Japan: PSE, VCCI
   - ✓ Australia: RCM
   - ✓ Global Air Travel: IATA battery rules (if applicable)
3. Review a preliminary budget allocation:
   - Testing & Certification budget: $______
   - Materials budget: $______
   - Manufacturing setup: $______
   - Regulatory consulting: $______
4. Set target retail price (affects material choices, certification depth, and profit margin)

**Comedic Elements:**
- Marketing team slides with absurd claims ("Our smart speaker will CHANGE YOUR LIFE through the power of SOUND")
- Legal team annotations in red ink on those slides ("Cannot claim life-changing properties without clinical trials - REMOVED")
- An overly enthusiastic intern who suggests selling the product in North Korea, triggering a mandatory compliance training pop-up

**Educational Content:**
- Brief explanation of each market's regulatory framework
- Interactive map where hovering over countries shows requirement summaries
- Key vocabulary introduced: "directive," "standard," "certification body"

### Phase 2: Initial Design & Prototype (4-6 minutes)

**Objective:** Build your first prototype by making material and component choices.

**Player Actions:**
1. **Material Selection** — For each major component, choose from 3-4 options with visible trade-offs:

   *Example: Enclosure Material for Smart Speaker*
   | Material | Cost | Fire Rating | Recyclability | Acoustic Properties | Aesthetics | Prop 65 Risk |
   |----------|------|-------------|---------------|---------------------|------------|--------------|
   | ABS Plastic | $ | UL94 HB (poor) | Low | Good | Matte finish | None |
   | PC/ABS Blend | $$ | UL94 V-0 (good) | Low | Good | Glossy | None |
   | Bamboo Composite | $$$ | Needs treatment | High (marketing!) | Excellent | Premium | Natural adhesive concerns |
   | Recycled Aluminum | $$$$ | Excellent | High | Requires damping | Premium | None |

   *Students must weigh: cost vs. certification pass likelihood vs. sustainability claims vs. aesthetics*

2. **Component Sourcing** — Select suppliers for electronic components:
   - Each supplier has a "Compliance Rating" (1-5 stars)
   - Low-rated suppliers are cheaper but may have fake certifications
   - High-rated suppliers provide complete documentation packets

3. **Manufacturing Process Selection** — Choose how the product will be made:
   - Injection molding (setup cost: high, per-unit: low, precision: high)
   - 3D printing (setup cost: low, per-unit: high, suitable for prototypes)
   - CNC machining (setup cost: medium, per-unit: medium, material options: wide)
   - The manufacturing choice affects which standards apply and what documentation is needed

4. **Submit Initial Design** — Generate a "Preliminary Technical File" that summarizes all choices

**Comedic Elements:**
- Steve the engineer's "creative" component suggestions ("I found these batteries from a surplus auction, they're FINE probably")
- A supplier catalog entry for "DEFINITELY CERTIFIED ELECTRONICS CO." with suspiciously misspelled certifications
- Your office plant has died because everyone's been too busy, and Janet from Marketing sends a passive-aggressive email about it

**Educational Content:**
- Material property comparisons (tensile strength, flammability rating, thermal conductivity)
- Introduction to manufacturing process selection criteria
- Supplier vetting and supply chain basics

### Phase 3: Pre-Certification Testing (5-8 minutes)

**Objective:** Run internal tests to predict certification outcomes before submitting to official bodies.

**Player Actions:**
1. **Allocate Test Budget** — Choose which tests to run internally:
   - Full EMC pre-scan ($2,500, 2 days)
   - Flammability test ($800, 1 day)
   - Mechanical stress test ($1,200, 1 day)
   - Chemical composition analysis ($3,000, 3 days)
   - Battery safety simulation ($4,000, 4 days)
   - Skip testing and submit directly (risky, saves time/money)

2. **Interactive Test Mini-Games** — Each test is a small interactive experience:
   
   *EMC Test Mini-Game:* A spectrum analyzer display shows frequency spikes. The player must identify which component is causing emissions above the limit line by clicking on "suspect frequencies" and being presented with potential sources. Drag ferrite beads, shielding, or filters onto the problem areas.

   *Flammability Test Mini-Game:* A slow-motion video of a flame test. The player must identify if the material self-extinguishes within the required time by watching the flame front and clicking "PASS" or "FAIL" at the right moment. Too early or too late and you get it wrong.

   *Mechanical Test Mini-Game:* A drop test simulation showing stress points. Players adjust drop height, surface type, and impact angle, then watch a physics simulation showing where the product cracks. Identify weak points on a 2D cross-section.

3. **Review Test Results** — Receive a test report with clear pass/fail/conditional results:
   - Red failures require design changes
   - Yellow conditionals can be accepted with proper documentation
   - Green passes are ready for submission

**Comedic Elements:**
- The test lab sends back photos of your product in increasingly absurd situations ("We also tested it in a wind tunnel at 200mph. It did not survive. We were curious.")
- A rival company's product accidentally gets tested alongside yours, and their results are hilariously bad
- Your intern accidentally drops the prototype during the "informal pre-pre-test" and the entire office freezes

**Educational Content:**
- Understanding test standards and what they measure
- Interpreting technical test results
- Cost-benefit analysis of testing strategies

### Phase 4: Certification Submission & Regulatory Navigation (6-10 minutes)

**Objective:** Navigate the actual submission process and respond to regulatory feedback.

**Player Actions:**
1. **Document Assembly** — A "Papers Please" style verification desk:
   - Stack of required forms appears on screen
   - Each document has fields that must be checked against your technical file
   - A bureaucrat's window slides documents back with "DISCREPANCY" stamps
   - Example discrepancy: "Form 12-A lists power supply as 5V/2A. Your schematic shows 5V/2.1A. INCONSISTENCY."
   - Player must decide: Resubmit corrected document (cost: $150, 1 day) or argue it's within tolerance (risk: full rejection)

2. **Regulatory Correspondence** — Incoming letters from certification bodies:
   - Written in dense bureaucratic language
   - A "Translate" button costs $200 (Gunther's time) and highlights the key issues
   - Players can attempt to interpret without translation (risk/reward)
   - Multiple rounds of correspondence may be needed

3. **In-Person Audit Preparation** (for harder products) — A surprise notification:
   - "A representative from the certification body will visit your facility in 3 days"
   - Player must prepare:
     - Assign a staff member to be "Quality Manager" for the day
     - Ensure all documentation is organized
     - Hide any non-compliant prototypes
     - Brief staff on what to say (and what NOT to say)
   - The audit is a dialogue-tree event where wrong answers trigger findings

4. **Non-Conformance Resolution** — When failures occur:
   - Root cause analysis mini-game: A fishbone diagram appears with blank sections
   - Player drags potential causes into categories (Materials, Methods, Machines, Manpower, Measurement, Environment)
   - Correct identification reduces the cost/time of the fix

**Comedic Elements:**
- Bureaucrat names: "Gunther von Compliance-Hausen," "Brenda Clipboard," "Mr. Zhang from the Ministry of Being Very Thorough"
- Absurd regulatory interpretations ("Your smart speaker is technically a 'sound reproducing device' AND a 'data processing unit' AND potentially a 'medical device' if someone uses it to play meditation music")
- An auto-reply from a regulatory body: "Your inquiry is very important to us. Current response time: 47 business days."
- Janet from Marketing bursts in: "WHAT DO YOU MEAN WE CAN'T SHIP? I ALREADY PROMISED INFLUENCERS!"

**Educational Content:**
- Understanding regulatory documentation
- Professional communication with certification bodies
- Root cause analysis methodology
- Audit preparation and quality management basics

### Phase 5: Manufacturing & Quality Control (4-6 minutes)

**Objective:** Set up production and ensure ongoing compliance.

**Player Actions:**
1. **Factory Setup** — Choose manufacturing partners:
   - Each factory has ratings for: Quality, Speed, Cost, Compliance History
   - Compliance History matters: a factory with violations may ship non-conforming products

2. **Production Batch Review** — First article inspection:
   - A "spot the difference" game between the certified design and production samples
   - Issues like: wrong screw type, different shade of plastic, missing label, slightly different component
   - Player must accept (ship with risk) or reject (delay for rework) each finding

3. **Label & Documentation Design** — Create the final product labels:
   - Drag-and-drop certification marks onto the product (CE, FCC, UL, etc.)
   - Warnings must be placed correctly (choking hazard for toys, Prop 65 warnings, etc.)
   - User manual includes required safety information
   - Missing or incorrect marks trigger a rejection at customs

4. **Shipment Preparation** — Final compliance check:
   - Bill of lading, certificate of conformity, test reports all verified
   - A customs simulation where missing paperwork causes delays

**Comedic Elements:**
- The factory sends a photo: "We made a small improvement to your design!" — they've added unnecessary LEDs that blink
- A shipment of "CE" stickers arrives, and they're misspelled "EC" — do you use them or wait?
- Your warehouse manager emails: "The pallets are ISPM 15 certified! ...wait, what's ISPM 15?"

**Educational Content:**
- Quality control processes
- Labeling requirements across markets
- Supply chain documentation
- First article inspection procedures

### Phase 6: Market Launch & Post-Market Surveillance (3-5 minutes)

**Objective:** Launch the product and handle what happens after.

**Player Actions:**
1. **Launch Day** — A narrative summary of the launch:
   - Sales figures based on how well certification went (delays reduce market share)
   - Revenue calculation showing profit after all the certification costs
   - Customer reviews appear (influenced by quality choices)

2. **Field Issues** — Post-market problems arrive:
   - A customer complaint about a burning smell (thermal issue)
   - A retailer in Germany notices missing WEEE registration
   - A competitor files a complaint with the FCC about interference
   - Each requires a response decision: recall, field fix, documentation update, or ignore (with consequences)

3. **Product Scorecard** — Final evaluation:
   - Certification Success Rate: % of markets achieved
   - Time to Market: days from start to launch
   - Budget Efficiency: % of budget remaining
   - Customer Satisfaction: based on quality choices
   - Sustainability Score: based on material choices
   - Overall Grade: A+ through F

4. **Unlock Next Product** — Based on performance:
   - Better budget for next product if profitable
   - More staff options if successful
   - New regulatory challenges unlocked

**Comedic Elements:**
- An Amazon review from "DefinitelyNotYourCompetitor" giving 1 star for absurd reasons
- A viral TikTok shows your product being used completely incorrectly but entertainingly
- Your legal team's increasingly desperate emails about a class-action lawsuit over "the thing with the thing"

**Educational Content:**
- Post-market surveillance obligations
- Recall procedures and decision-making
- Customer feedback integration
- Business metrics and profitability

## 4. EXPANDED GAME MECHANICS FOR DEPTH

### 4.1 Decision Consequence Web

Every major decision creates branches that affect later phases:

```
Initial Material Choice
├── Cheap ABS → Passes cost target
│   ├── Fails flammability → Must add flame retardant ($, time)
│   │   ├── Flame retardant triggers Prop 65 → Warning label required
│   │   └── OR switch to inherently FR material ($$)
│   └── OR accept flammability risk and ship to lenient markets only
├── Premium PC/ABS → Over budget
│   ├── Janet demands feature cuts to compensate
│   └── OR increase retail price (market competitiveness drops)
└── Sustainable choice → Marketing boost
    ├── Certification takes longer (new material requires extra testing)
    └── BUT unlocks "Eco-Certified" badge for premium pricing
```

### 4.2 Staff Morale & Events

Staff members have a hidden "Morale" meter:
- High morale: occasional bonuses (Gunther translates for free, Steve works overtime)
- Low morale: staff make mistakes, quit, or sabotage subtly
- Events affect morale:
  - Choosing their recommendation: +Morale
  - Rejecting their advice repeatedly: -Morale
  - Office perks (can be purchased from budget): +Morale
  - Crunch time during certification: -Morale

### 4.3 Competitor Pressure System

A visible competitor timeline shows "GloboCorp" or "TechSpan" developing similar products:
- If they launch first, your market share drops
- Creates time pressure without being punishing
- Can gather intelligence on competitors (costs money, provides hints about certification shortcuts)

### 4.4 "Regulatory Library" Unlock System

As students encounter regulations, they're added to an in-game reference:
- Accessible anytime
- Written in student-friendly language with real regulation numbers
- Serves as a study resource for DP assessments
- Pops up "Did You Know?" facts during loading screens

### 4.5 Humor Systems

**Email Inbox:** Throughout the game, absurd emails arrive:
- "RE: Urgent: Your product's carbon footprint has offended a small nation"
- "FWD: FWD: FWD: IMPORTANT - Is our product vegan? A influencer is asking"
- "Your certification auditor's lunch order (they're vegan, gluten-free, and allergic to bureaucracy)"

**Office Events:**
- The office printer displays "PC LOAD LETTER" but it's actually "CE LOAD LETTER" and everyone panics
- Bring Your Child to Work Day happens during a critical audit
- The fire alarm goes off because someone microwaved fish in the break room test lab

## 5. TECHNICAL ARCHITECTURE PLAN

### 5.1 Technology Stack Recommendation

**For Web Deployment (Educational Site):**

| Layer | Technology | Rationale |
|-------|------------|-----------|
| Frontend Framework | React + TypeScript | Component reusability, type safety for complex state |
| State Management | Zustand or Redux Toolkit | Complex game state across phases |
| UI Components | Custom CSS + Framer Motion | Animations for document shuffling, test mini-games |
| Game Engine Elements | Phaser.js (for mini-games) or pure Canvas | Test simulations, visual product builder |
| Backend (if saving) | Firebase or Supabase | Student progress saving, teacher dashboard |
| Build Tool | Vite | Fast development, good React integration |

**Alternative: Single-Page Vanilla Approach**
- Pure HTML/CSS/JS with a game state object
- Easier to deploy on any educational platform
- Fewer dependencies
- Good for a smaller scope if the team is small

### 5.2 Core Data Structures

```typescript
// The central game state
interface GameState {
  currentCharacter: CharacterId; // 'mina' | 'leo' | 'samira'
  currentProductIndex: number; // 0, 1, or 2
  currentPhase: PhaseId; // 'brief' | 'design' | 'testing' | 'certification' | 'manufacturing' | 'launch'
  budget: number;
  reputation: number;
  staffMorale: Record<string, number>;
  inventory: InventoryItem[];
  unlockedRegulations: string[];
  completedCertifications: Certification[];
  timeline: GameEvent[];
  competitorProgress: number; // 0-100
}

// Product definition
interface Product {
  id: string;
  name: string;
  components: Component[];
  targetMarkets: Market[];
  selectedMaterials: Record<string, MaterialId>;
  selectedSuppliers: Record<string, SupplierId>;
  testResults: TestResult[];
  certificationStatus: Record<string, CertificationStatus>;
  issues: ProductIssue[];
}

// A component that needs material selection
interface Component {
  id: string;
  name: string;
  materialOptions: MaterialOption[];
  selectedMaterial: MaterialId | null;
  regulatoryRequirements: RegulationRef[];
}

// Material option with trade-offs
interface MaterialOption {
  id: string;
  name: string;
  cost: number;
  properties: Record<string, number>; // e.g., { flammabilityRating: 2, tensileStrength: 85, recyclability: 3 }
  certifications: string[]; // Pre-approved for certain standards
  prop65Risk: boolean;
  sustainabilityScore: number;
  manufacturingCompatible: ManufacturingProcess[];
}

// Regulatory requirement
interface RegulationRef {
  standardId: string; // e.g., "FCC_PART_15"
  market: string; // e.g., "USA"
  testRequired: string; // e.g., "emc_emissions"
  passThreshold: number;
}

// Test result
interface TestResult {
  testId: string;
  standardRef: string;
  status: 'pass' | 'fail' | 'conditional';
  score: number;
  details: string;
  recommendations: string[];
}
```

### 5.4 Mini-Game Technical Designs

**EMC Spectrum Analyzer Mini-Game:**
- SVG-based spectrum display
- Animated noise floor with peaks representing emissions
- Red limit line drawn based on selected standard (FCC, CE, etc.)
- Clickable frequency peaks that highlight and show: "Possible source: Switching regulator U3, 700kHz harmonic"
- Drag-and-drop mitigation components: ferrite bead, shield can, filter capacitor
- Each mitigation applied reduces the peak height with an animation
- Success when all peaks are below the limit line

**Document Verification Mini-Game (Papers Please Style):**
- Split screen: left side is your technical file, right side is the submission form
- Fields glow red when there's a discrepancy
- Player must click the discrepancy, see the two conflicting values side by side
- Choose action from a dropdown: "Correct form" (costs time/fee), "Correct technical file" (may require redesign), "Override with justification" (risky)
- A "stamp" animation plays: green APPROVED stamp or red REJECTED stamp
- Bureaucrat character appears with voice bubble commentary

**Drop Test Simulation:**
- 2D cross-section view of the product with material layers color-coded
- Stress points calculated from material properties selected in Phase 2
- When "dropped," a physics animation shows crack propagation
- Player identifies the component that failed and selects a fix: thicker wall, different material, added rib, changed geometry
- Each fix has cost and weight implications

## 6. CONTENT PIPELINE

### 6.1 Writing Requirements

For each character path (3) × each product (3) × each phase (6) = 54 unique content nodes

Each node requires:
- **Narrative introduction:** 200-400 words setting the scene with character voice
- **Decision options:** 3-5 meaningful choices with different outcomes
- **Feedback text:** Pass/fail/conditional results with humor
- **Staff dialogue:** 2-4 character-specific lines per phase
- **Email content:** 2-5 emails per phase
- **Educational popup triggers:** 1-3 per phase linking to regulatory library

**Total writing estimate:** ~40,000-60,000 words across all content

## 9. SAMPLE PLAYTHROUGH: Lumina Smart Speaker

Here's what the first product experience looks like:

**Minutes 0-3 (Phase 1 - Brief):**
Mina reads the product concept. The team wants a smart speaker with voice assistant, good audio quality, and a distinctive design. Target price: $79.99. Markets selected: USA and EU. Budget set at $150,000 total.

**Minutes 3-8 (Phase 2 - Design):**
Player selects PC/ABS blend for the enclosure (good fire rating, moderate cost), chooses a Class D amplifier module from a 5-star rated supplier, and selects injection molding. Steve suggests using a cheaper power supply from "Shenzhen Bargain Electronics" (2-star rating). The player declines and chooses a UL-listed supply from a reputable supplier.

**Minutes 8-15 (Phase 3 - Testing):**
Player runs EMC pre-scan and discovers the WiFi module is emitting above FCC limits at 2.4GHz. The mini-game requires adding shielding and a ferrite bead. Flammability test passes. Mechanical test shows the enclosure cracks at a thin section around the USB port — player adds a rib to the design (adds $0.15 per unit, adds 1 day).

**Minutes 15-23 (Phase 4 - Certification):**
Documents submitted to FCC and EU Notified Body. FCC comes back with a request for additional photos of the test setup. EU reviewer flags that the power supply's CE mark is from a suspended certification body (a real problem!). Player must source a new power supply from the approved list, costing 2 days and $2,000 in rush fees. An audit is scheduled. Janet role-plays as the auditor to help prep, but her questions are all about marketing claims and completely unhelpful.

**Minutes 23-28 (Phase 5 - Manufacturing):**
Factory selected in Vietnam with good ratings. First article inspection reveals the silk-screened logo is 2mm off-center. Player accepts it (minor). CE and FCC marks are correctly placed on the bottom label. Shipment documentation verified.

**Minutes 28-35 (Phase 6 - Launch):**
Product launches 3 weeks late due to the power supply issue. Market share is 65% of what it could have been. Revenue: $480,000 against $128,000 in costs. Profit: $352,000. Customer reviews are good (4.2 stars) but some complain about the "boring" design — Marcus is devastated. Product 2 (LuminaBuds) unlocked with a $200,000 budget increase.
